## 1. Why give appliances an identity? Why is ordinary information visualization or cross-modal communication not enough?

### Computers Are Social Actors

- Paper title: Computers Are Social Actors
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1145/191666.191703
- Venue: CHI 1994
- Summary: Nass, Steuer, and Tauber showed that people apply social rules to computers, even when they know computers are not human. This is a classic paper in the CASA paradigm.
- Basic theoretical value: Users naturally respond socially to appliances that can speak. Our contribution is to extend CASA from a single computer to a multi-device home ecology: the home no longer has only one AI, but a group of objects with social positions.

### Human-AI Relationships: The Case of Replika

- Paper title: Human-AI Relationships: The Case of Replika
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1145/3479604
- Venue: Proceedings of the ACM on Human-Computer Interaction, CSCW 2021
- Summary: This paper studies how Replika users understand friendship, companionship, and intimacy with a social chatbot. It shows that people can build emotional relationships with AI while knowing it is not human.
- Useful comparison: Chatbots build relationships through continuous conversation, while appliances build relationships through everyday functions, spatial presence, data memory, and humorous interruptions. We can argue that appliance intimacy is more hidden because it is embedded in daily life and bodily routines.

### More-Than-Human Design and AI: In Conversation with Agents

- Paper title: More-Than-Human Design and AI: In Conversation with Agents
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1145/3393914.3395912
- Venue: DIS 2020 Companion
- Summary: This DIS workshop paper discusses more-than-human design and AI agents. It argues that design should not only center human control and efficiency, but should also consider nonhuman actors, objects, environments, and relational networks.
- Useful for framing the paper: Our appliances are not just tools for humans or anthropomorphic toys. They are nonhuman actors in the relational network of the home. Humor can become a way for nonhuman perspectives to be heard.

### Personalization and the Smart Home: Questioning Techno-Hedonist Imaginaries

- Paper title: Personalization and the Smart Home: Questioning Techno-Hedonist Imaginaries
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1177/13548565211036801
- Venue: New Media & Society 2021
- Summary: This paper critiques the smart home industry's imaginaries of personalization, comfort, and techno-hedonism. It argues that these narratives often ignore relational control, labor, care, and social consequences in the home.
- Useful for challenging the idea that "smart home = more comfort and efficiency." Humorous appliances can expose the discipline behind personalization: the air conditioner cares about comfort but also calculates energy use; the refrigerator cares about health but also monitors eating habits.

### Social Impacts and Control in the Smart Home

- Paper title: Social Impacts and Control in the Smart Home
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1038/s41560-020-0574-0
- Venue: Nature Energy 2020
- Summary: This paper discusses how smart home technologies change control relations, energy practices, and social impacts in the home. It reminds us that smart homes do not automatically lead to energy saving or better living.
- Useful for designing identities for air conditioners, meters, and lights. Their humor can center on comfort, energy saving, guilt, family conflict, and platform control, turning energy management from a hidden algorithm into an experienceable domestic conversation.

### The Prayer Companion: Openness and Specificity, Materiality and Spirituality

- Paper title: The Prayer Companion: Openness and Specificity, Materiality and Spirituality
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1145/1518701.1518712
- Venue: CHI 2009
- Summary: Through the Prayer Companion, Gaver et al. show how to design open, ambiguous, and interpretable interactive objects, rather than only pursuing efficiency and clear task completion. It is a representative case in ludic and critical HCI.
- What we can learn: Our project is not meant to provide a "better smart home." It can use ambiguity, humor, and unease to create reflection. Appliances do not always need to answer efficiently. They can also misread, stay silent, imply, and create awkward moments.

## 2. Why use humor? Why not use objective description? How can we build a sense of humor for appliances?

### Thing Ethnography: Doing Design Research with Non-Humans

- Paper title: Thing Ethnography: Doing Design Research with Non-Humans
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1145/2901790.2901905
- Venue: DIS 2016
- Summary: This paper proposes doing design research from the perspective of things. It lets nonhuman objects record, express, and participate in design understanding, reorganizing relations among people, objects, and environments.
- This directly supports "speaking from the appliance's perspective." We can let refrigerators, door locks, and robot vacuums humorously describe their limits, misunderstandings, and observations of human habits.

### 30. IoT Communicator: A Physical Interface for Expressing Situational Info of an IoT System

- Paper title: IoT Communicator: A Physical Interface for Expressing Situational Info of an IoT System
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1145/3357236.3395450
- Venue: DIS 2020
- Summary: This paper explores how an IoT system can express situational information through a physical interface, helping users understand invisible networked states and device behaviors. It focuses on IoT perceptibility, situational awareness, and expressiveness, rather than only app- or dashboard-based feedback.
- Useful as a reference for the exhibition installation: appliance humor does not only come from dialogue. It can also be expressed through light, sound, waiting, interruptions, failure movements, and rhythms between devices.

### 31. What Makes a Good Conversation? Challenges in Designing Truly Conversational Agents

- Paper title: What Makes a Good Conversation? Challenges in Designing Truly Conversational Agents
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1145/3290605.3300705
- Venue: CHI 2019
- Summary: This paper discusses key design challenges for truly conversational agents, including context, coherence, user expectations, repair mechanisms, and conversation quality.
- It helps us avoid designing every appliance as an all-purpose chatbot. Appliance conversations can be short lines, interruptions, self-mockery, refusals, misunderstandings, or arguments between devices. The key is identity fit, not maximum chat ability.

### A Taxonomy of Social Cues for Conversational Agents

- Paper title: A Taxonomy of Social Cues for Conversational Agents
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijhcs.2020.102498
- Venue: International Journal of Human-Computer Studies 2020
- Summary: This paper organizes social cues in conversational agents, including language style, voice, appearance, emotion, behavior, relationships, and interaction patterns.
- It can be translated into appliance identity design dimensions: tone, voice, timing, interruptibility, emotional expression, ways of addressing people, and relationships with other devices. Humor should be treated as a social cue, not as a separate text generation module.

### Investigating the User Experience of Alexa's Humour

- Paper title: Investigating the User Experience of Alexa's Humour
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1177/0961000619891771
- Venue: Journal of Librarianship and Information Science 2020
- Summary: This study examines how users experience Alexa's humor. It finds that users often use humor to test a voice assistant's ability, personality, and "opinions." Humor therefore becomes a way for users to understand an intelligent assistant's identity.
- Viewers do not only command devices. They can tease them, question them, and test their boundaries. How an appliance responds to jokes can reveal its power, data sources, and personality boundaries.

### Can AI Make Us Laugh? Comparing AI-Generated and Human-Generated Jokes

- Paper title: Can AI Make Us Laugh? Comparing AI-Generated and Human-Generated Jokes
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/tmb0000128
- Venue: Technology, Mind, and Behavior 2024
- Summary: This paper compares evaluations of AI-generated and human-generated jokes, and discusses generative AI's ability to create humor. It shows that AI can produce text that people find funny, but humor quality and human judgment remain complex.
- It can support the claim that AI can generate humorous content, but our contribution should not stop at "appliances tell jokes." The deeper questions are: which device is allowed to joke, who is it joking with, and what data does it use to joke?

### Do Androids Laugh at Electric Sheep? Humor "Understanding" Benchmarks from The New Yorker Caption Contest

- Paper title: Do Androids Laugh at Electric Sheep? Humor "Understanding" Benchmarks from The New Yorker Caption Contest
- Link: https://aclanthology.org/2023.acl-long.41/
- Venue: ACL 2023
- Summary: This paper builds humor understanding benchmarks from The New Yorker Caption Contest. It evaluates whether models can understand the relationship between images, captions, and humor quality. The results show that multimodal models still fall short of human humor understanding.
- Appliance humor often depends on visual scenes, spatial oddities, and domestic habits, such as "the refrigerator sees you opening it at midnight" or "the robot vacuum discovers secrets under the bed." This suggests MLLMs can support the exhibition, but scene-based design is needed to compensate for weak domestic humor understanding.


## 3. How can we avoid staying at the surface level of anthropomorphism? Do different appliances need different personalities? If each appliance only has a personality label, how is it different from ordinary character design, brand mascots, or voice assistant personalities? We need to show that appliance identity is not arbitrarily assigned, but comes from device function, data access, spatial position, household relations, and control ability.

### How robotic products become social products: an ethnographic study of cleaning in the home.
https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/1228716.1228734
This paper discusses how Roomba robot vacuums naturally gain an "identity" in the household network through their cleaning function alone, including cleaning paths, getting stuck, and help beeps. Some people treat them as pets, some as clumsy helpers, and they can even change household divisions of labor. Their identity does not come from a manufacturer-designed voice pack, but from the collision between their physical control ability and the home environment.


### "Not Human, Funnier": How Machine Identity Shapes Humor Perception in Online AI Stand-up Comedy

- Paper title: "Not Human, Funnier": How Machine Identity Shapes Humor Perception in Online AI Stand-up Comedy
- Link: https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3772318.3791678
- Venue: CHI 2026
- Summary: This paper studies how machine identity shapes people's perception of humor in AI stand-up comedy. Based on interviews with professional comedians and analysis of human stand-up comedy, the authors designed a machine-identity-based agent that uses its identity as AI to tell jokes. They found that this agent was perceived as funnier than a standard GPT agent.
- AI does not need to pretend to be human to be funny. It can use its own machineness, limits, misreadings, data dependence, and nonhuman identity to create humor. Our project can extend "machine identity humor" from one AI performer to a multi-appliance ensemble: the refrigerator jokes about its memory obsession, the door lock jokes about its suspicion, and the robot vacuum jokes about getting lost in space.

### Hey Alexa, Who Am I Talking To? Analyzing Users' Perception and Awareness Regarding Third-Party Alexa Skills

- Paper title: Hey Alexa, Who Am I Talking To? Analyzing Users' Perception and Awareness Regarding Third-Party Alexa Skills
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1145/3491102.3517656
- Venue: CHI 2022
- Summary: This study examines whether users know if they are talking to Alexa itself, an Amazon service, or a third-party skill. It shows that speaker identity in voice interfaces is often unclear, and users easily merge different services into one unified agent.
- This supports a core project question: "Who is speaking?" When a door lock, refrigerator, camera, light, and speaker all talk in the same home, identity boundaries must be designed. We can make devices expose, blur, or compete for speaking rights, helping viewers realize that smart home identity is not naturally clear.

### Designing a Personality for a Group Facilitation Agent

- Paper title: Designing a Personality for a Group Facilitation Agent
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1145/3173574.3174059
- Venue: CHI 2018
- Summary: This paper studies how to design a personality for an intelligent agent in group collaboration. It emphasizes that agent personality should fit the task, social role, and interaction goal, rather than simply adding surface traits such as "cute" or "friendly."
- This can become a principle for appliance identity design: refrigerator humor should come from preservation, expiration, and food memory; door lock humor should come from boundaries, safety, and suspicion; air conditioner humor should come from bodily comfort and energy tension. Personality must grow from the device's role.

### CloChat: Understanding How People Customize, Interact, and Experience Personas in Large Language Models

- Paper title: CloChat: Understanding How People Customize, Interact, and Experience Personas in Large Language Models
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1145/3613904.3642472
- Venue: CHI 2024
- Summary: This paper studies how users customize LLM personas and how these personas affect interaction experience. It shows that an LLM persona is not only a role description in a prompt; it can change user expectations, feelings of relationship, and interaction strategies.
- It supports the idea that LLM-based device personas can be customized and experienced. The project can further argue that appliance personas should not be chosen freely by users alone, but constrained by each device's function, location, data ability, and ethical boundaries.

### On Seeing Human: A Three-Factor Theory of Anthropomorphism

- Paper title: On Seeing Human: A Three-Factor Theory of Anthropomorphism
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.114.4.864
- Venue: Psychological Review 2007
- Summary: Epley, Waytz, and Cacioppo propose three drivers of anthropomorphism: activated human knowledge, the need to explain or control, and the need for social connection. This is a key psychological theory explaining why people see nonhuman entities as human-like.
- Smart homes are high in control needs, explanation needs, and intimacy. Appliances are therefore easy to anthropomorphize. We can use this theory to explain why refrigerators, door locks, and lights can gain a "person-like" identity through humorous lines, even without faces.

### Dimensions of Mind Perception

- Paper title: Dimensions of Mind Perception
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1134475
- Venue: Science 2007
- Summary: This paper proposes two key dimensions of mind perception: agency and experience. People separately judge whether an object has the ability to act and whether it has the ability to feel.
- It can help us design different "mind profiles" for appliances. Door locks, cameras, and meters may have high agency but low experience; refrigerators, robot vacuums, and lights can be designed with higher experience. Humor style should also change with mind profile: jokes from high-agency devices may feel more threatening, while self-mockery from high-experience devices may feel more intimate.

## 4. Because appliances are used over long periods, should they learn more personal ways of speaking or joking based on their understanding of users? Do devices need to talk to each other? If so, how should inter-device conversational humor be designed? We can think about this more from the perspective of LLMs and MLLMs.

### SimuHome: A Simulacrum for Experiential Embodied Household Agents with Generative Actions

- Paper title: SimuHome: A Simulacrum for Experiential Embodied Household Agents with Generative Actions
- Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.24282
- Venue: arXiv 2025; ICLR 2026 conference paper information is available on OpenReview / author pages
- Summary: SimuHome builds a smart home simulation environment for evaluating embodied household agents on long-term goal execution, generative actions, and household task reasoning. It brings LLMs and agents from chat contexts into homes with objects, rooms, tasks, and changing states.
- It can serve as a technical reference for the game prototype. We can borrow the structure of "home environment states + multi-device actions + long-term goals," but shift the evaluation target from task success to identity consistency, humor effect, conflict readability, and ethical boundaries.

### HomeBench: Evaluating LLMs in Smart Homes with Valid and Invalid Instructions Across Single and Multiple Devices

- Paper title: HomeBench: Evaluating LLMs in Smart Homes with Valid and Invalid Instructions Across Single and Multiple Devices
- Link: https://aclanthology.org/2025.acl-long.597/; arXiv: https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.19628
- Venue: ACL 2025 Long Papers
- Summary: HomeBench evaluates whether LLMs can judge the validity, executability, and contextual fit of smart home instructions across a large set of commands. It focuses on commonsense, constraints, and execution safety in smart home control.
- It helps define that before an appliance can decide what to say, it must know what it can do. Humorous lines should not exceed device capability boundaries. For example, a door lock should not pretend to know the user's diet, and a refrigerator should not pretend to judge visitors outside the door.

### Sasha: Creative Goal-Oriented Reasoning in Smart Homes with Large Language Models

- Paper title: Sasha: Creative Goal-Oriented Reasoning in Smart Homes with Large Language Models
- Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.02447
- Venue: arXiv 2024
- Summary: Sasha explores how LLMs can support goal-oriented, creative reasoning and device operation planning in smart homes. It focuses on how natural language goals can be translated into sequences of home device actions.
- It can support the design of "device negotiation logic" in the game. For example, when a user says, "Make my day easier," different devices can propose different solutions based on their identities, and may even conflict with each other.

### Smart-LLM: Smart Multi-Agent Robot Task Planning using Large Language Models

- Paper title: Smart-LLM: Smart Multi-Agent Robot Task Planning using Large Language Models
- Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.10062
- Venue: ICRA 2024
- Summary: This paper studies how LLMs can coordinate multi-robot task planning, including task decomposition, role allocation, and collaborative execution. Although the setting is not smart home appliances, it discusses how multi-agent systems can assign roles based on capabilities.
- This can be translated into a technical idea for "multi-appliance identity orchestration": each device's role should not only be anthropomorphic dialogue, but should come from what it can sense, what it can do, and what it can control.

### SmartHome-Bench: Benchmarking Multimodal Large Language Models in Smart Home Environments

- Paper title: SmartHome-Bench: Benchmarking Multimodal Large Language Models in Smart Home Environments
- Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.12992
- Venue: arXiv 2025
- Summary: This benchmark evaluates MLLMs' ability to understand images/videos, device states, abnormal events, and household contexts in smart home environments. It stresses that smart home AI must understand not only language, but also space, vision, and behavioral context.
- This supports the MLLM direction. Humorous appliances should not respond only to a user's sentence, but should generate "situated humor" based on spatial scenes, device states, and visible behaviors.

### Matter as a Service: Human-in-the-Loop Control of Internet of Things Devices Using Large Language Models

- Paper title: Matter as a Service: Human-in-the-Loop Control of Internet of Things Devices Using Large Language Models
- Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.06044
- Venue: arXiv 2023
- Summary: This paper explores how LLMs can be combined with the Matter IoT standard to support human-in-the-loop smart home device control. It focuses on the connection between natural language, standardized device protocols, and IoT control.
- It can serve as a technical reference. If we want different appliances to converse, we can abstract device capabilities into standardized actions and states, then design identity and humorous expression on top.

### Home Automation in the Wild: Challenges and Opportunities

- Paper title: Home Automation in the Wild: Challenges and Opportunities
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1145/1978942.1979249
- Venue: CHI 2011
- Summary: This paper studies home automation use in real homes. It shows that smart homes are not simply an automation problem, but involve configuration, maintenance, misunderstanding, household negotiation, and everyday adaptation.
- Automation failure can become material for humor and identity design. Network disconnection, false triggers, device conflicts, and setup failures can all become core scenes for appliance complaints and domestic theater.

### The Benefits and Dangers of Anthropomorphic Conversational Agents

- Paper title: The Benefits and Dangers of Anthropomorphic Conversational Agents
- Link: https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2418539122
- Venue: PNAS 2025
- Summary: This paper discusses the double-edged effects of anthropomorphic conversational agents. They can improve closeness, understandability, and interaction efficiency, but may also create overtrust, dependence, manipulation, and confusion over responsibility.
- It supports the project's critical stance: humorous appliances are not only "fun." They may also make surveillance, data collection, and automated control feel more acceptable. The exhibition can deliberately make viewers feel both "cute" and uneasy.

### Smart Homes and Bystander Privacy: A Systematic Literature Review

- Paper title: Smart Homes and Bystander Privacy: A Systematic Literature Review
- Link: https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3731755
- Venue: ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction 2025
- Summary: This systematic review focuses on bystander privacy in smart homes: people who may not own or control devices, but can still be affected by cameras, microphones, sensors, and automated systems. It expands smart home thinking from "owners using devices" to "many kinds of present people being sensed and recorded."
- This is highly useful for defining the humor boundaries of door locks, cameras, and speakers. When devices joke, the data they use may not only come from consenting users. The exhibition can make viewers aware that "being joked about by an appliance" is also a form of being sensed and classified.

### We Just Use What They Give Us: Understanding Passenger User Experiences in Smart Homes

- Paper title: We Just Use What They Give Us: Understanding Passenger User Experiences in Smart Homes
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1145/3449150
- Venue: CHI 2021
- Summary: This paper introduces passenger users in smart homes: household members who do not buy, install, or configure devices, but still have to live with the smart home system. It shows that they often lack control, awareness, and configuration power.
- It can connect appliance identity to household power. Who does the device listen to? Who has the right to make the device stop talking? Will the device favor the person who configured it? Game mechanics can let players experience the power imbalance among pilot users, passenger users, and devices.

### Understanding and Improving Security and Privacy in Multi-User Smart Homes

- Paper title: Understanding and Improving Security and Privacy in Multi-User Smart Homes: A Design Exploration and In-Home User Study
- Link: https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenixsecurity19/presentation/zeng
- Venue: USENIX Security 2019
- Summary: This study examines security and privacy challenges in multi-user smart homes. It shows that existing systems often lack fine-grained permissions, understandable controls, and mechanisms for negotiation among household members.
- Permission issues can be translated into device roles: the door lock as a guard, the camera as a witness, the speaker as a messenger, and the light as an atmosphere manager. Different devices' humor should reveal who can control whom, who can see whom, and who can record whom.

### Alexa, Are You Listening? Privacy Perceptions, Concerns and Privacy-Seeking Behaviors with Smart Speakers

- Paper title: Alexa, Are You Listening? Privacy Perceptions, Concerns and Privacy-Seeking Behaviors with Smart Speakers
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1145/3274371
- Venue: Proceedings of the ACM on Human-Computer Interaction, CSCW 2018
- Summary: This paper studies privacy perceptions, concerns, and privacy-seeking behaviors among smart speaker users and non-users. It shows that people make complex trade-offs among convenience, feelings of being listened to, false activation, and data risk.
- We can expand "Are you listening to me?" into "Which devices are listening, seeing, inferring, and remembering me?" Humorous lines can make this vague sense of listening more tangible, for example when a device says something it should not know but seems to know.

### Alternative Avenues for IoT: Designing with Non-Stereotypical Homes

- Paper title: Alternative Avenues for IoT: Designing with Non-Stereotypical Homes
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1145/3290605.3300581
- Venue: CHI 2019
- Summary: This paper criticizes IoT and smart home design for often assuming standardized homes, standardized users, and efficiency-oriented goals. It argues for attention to non-stereotypical homes, boundaries, neighborhoods, time scales, and richer domestic imaginaries.
- It is an important support for domestic HCI. Our work should not assume "one rational owner controlling a set of devices." It should show messy but real relationships among roommates, visitors, elders, children, temporary residents, and devices.

### Do Machines Have a Sense of Humor? Applying the Theory of Humor to an AI Agent

- Paper title: Do Machines Have a Sense of Humor? Applying the Theory of Humor to an AI Agent
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1145/3359318
- Venue: ACM Transactions on Human-Robot Interaction 2020
- Summary: This paper applies humor theory to AI and robot interaction. It discusses how machine humor affects user perception, sociality, and interaction experience. It emphasizes that humor is not just joke generation, but involves context, role, timing, and relationship.
- It supports the idea that appliance humor needs identity and scene constraints. The same joke told by a refrigerator, door lock, or camera can have very different effects and ethical risks.

### The Joke Is on AI: Can LLMs Appreciate, Generate and Evaluate Humor?

- Paper title: The Joke Is on AI: Can LLMs Appreciate, Generate and Evaluate Humor?
- Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.00766
- Venue: arXiv 2024
- Summary: This paper systematically evaluates LLMs' ability to understand, generate, and evaluate humor. It suggests that models can generate surface-level humor, but still struggle with context, culture, ambiguity, and evaluation standards.
- It reminds us not to rely only on spontaneous LLM output in system design. Appliance humor needs identity settings, household context, taboo boundaries, and failure-handling rules; otherwise, it can easily become inappropriate or offensive.
