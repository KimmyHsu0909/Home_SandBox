# Device Persona / AI Identity Related Paper Summaries

## 1. Products as Agents: Metaphors for Designing the Products of the IoT Age

**Paper Link:**  
https://doi.org/10.1145/3025453.3025797  
https://www.researchgate.net/publication/316650808_Products_as_Agents_Metaphors_for_Designing_the_Products_of_the_IoT_Age

**Paper Summary:**  
This CHI 2017 paper argues that products in the IoT era are no longer merely passive tools. Instead, they increasingly possess a degree of agency: they can sense data, interpret contexts, take action, and influence relationships between people and things. The authors propose understanding smart products as agents and distinguish different forms of product agency, such as Collectors that gather information, Actors that autonomously respond or intervene, and Creators that generate new possibilities. The focus is not on simply anthropomorphizing products, but on helping designers understand the action capacity, scope of influence, and responsibility boundaries of intelligent products.

**What We Can Learn:**  
This paper can serve as a theoretical foundation for designing AI Identity in smart home devices. Its agency framework can help us distinguish different levels of device identity and action. Some devices may mainly observe and remind, some may actively adjust the environment, and others may participate in generating new lifestyle solutions. The paper also reminds us that device identity should not be reduced to personality traits. It should also define what the device can do, when it can proactively intervene, who it affects, and how it explains its actions.

## 2. Thing Ethnography: Doing Design Research with Non-Humans

**Paper Link:**  
https://doi.org/10.1145/2901790.2901905  
https://research.hva.nl/en/publications/thing-ethnography-doing-design-research-with-non-humans/

**Paper Summary:**  
This DIS 2016 paper proposes thing ethnography, a design research approach that studies everyday practices from the perspective of things. By treating everyday objects as co-ethnographers, the paper challenges traditional human-centered design research methods. It argues that many domestic practices cannot be fully understood through user interviews alone, because the location, movement, relationships, neglect, and dependence surrounding objects also reveal the structure of everyday life.

**What We Can Learn:**  
This paper provides a methodological foundation for designing identity from the device's own perspective. When designing roles for smart home devices, we should not only ask what users want, but also ask what devices see, experience, and relate to within the home. For our project, cameras, lights, air conditioners, refrigerators, robot vacuums, and other devices can be treated as observers with different contextual perspectives, and these perspectives can become inputs for multi-agent interaction.

## 3. Thing-Centered Narratives: A Study of Object Personas

**Paper Link:**  
https://www.research.ed.ac.uk/en/publications/thing-centered-narratives-a-study-of-object-personas/

**Paper Summary:**  
This paper directly discusses object personas, or thing-centered narratives that construct personas for objects. The authors argue that objects not only reflect people's lifestyles, but also participate in shaping everyday practices. By constructing object personas, designers can describe an object's history, relationships, needs, emotions, use contexts, and connections with other objects. This helps reveal design opportunities that conventional user personas may overlook.

**What We Can Learn:**  
This paper is directly useful for our AI Identity design. It shows that personas do not have to belong only to users; they can also belong to devices or objects. We can borrow the object persona method to create identity cards for each smart home device, including its functional responsibility, location in the home, interaction tone, concerns, emotional tendencies, action boundaries, relationships with other devices, and how it understands different users. This can turn device identity from an abstract idea into an actionable design tool.

## 4. Making Everyday Things Talk: Speculative Conversations into the Future of Voice Interfaces at Home

**Paper Link:**  
https://doi.org/10.1145/3411763.3450390  
https://colab.ws/articles/10.1145%2F3411763.3450390

**Paper Summary:**  
This CHI 2021 Extended Abstracts paper explores what might happen if people interacted directly with everyday objects instead of communicating with all home devices through centralized voice assistants such as Alexa or Google Assistant. Using speculative conversations and Thing Interviews, the authors imagine how everyday objects such as boots, hygiene products, perfume bottles, and toilet paper might express their own perspectives, needs, and relationships with humans and other non-human entities. The paper focuses on conversational capabilities: what kinds of relationships, tones, and worldviews emerge when everyday things become speaking subjects.

**What We Can Learn:**  
This paper strongly supports our motivation to move from a centralized assistant model toward a model where individual devices speak for themselves. It suggests that smart home interaction does not need to be represented by a single unified assistant. Different devices can express different voices based on their location, function, and contextual experience. For our system, we can design mechanisms for conversations among devices, allowing refrigerators, lights, air conditioners, door locks, and other devices to become interaction participants with their own expressive styles and perspectives on domestic life.

## 5. Designing Personas for Expressive Robots: Personality in the New Breed of Moving, Speaking, and Colorful Social Home Robots

**Paper Link:**  
https://doi.org/10.1145/3424153  
https://discovery.ucl.ac.uk/id/eprint/10122969/

**Paper Summary:**  
This ACM THRI 2021 paper studies how to design personas for non-humanoid voice-based home robots. The authors designed three robot personas: Butler, Buddy, and Sidekick. These personas were expressed through multimodal cues such as language, tone of voice, color, and movement. The study used the Big Five personality framework to evaluate whether users could perceive the intended personality differences. The results show that even when a robot is not humanoid in appearance, users can still recognize personality traits through voice, motion, and visual feedback.

**What We Can Learn:**  
This paper helps us expand AI Identity beyond text prompts into multimodal expression. A device's identity is not only reflected in what it says, but also in how it says it, when it speaks, how proactive it is, what its voice sounds like, how its lights change, and whether its movements are restrained or lively. In a smart home context, lights can express state through brightness and color temperature, air conditioners can express care through airflow changes, and robot vacuums can express personality through movement paths and verbal prompts. The paper also reminds us that personas need to be accurately perceived by users, so future evaluation should test whether users can understand the intended device identities.

## 6. Proceed with Care: Reimagining Home IoT Through a Care Perspective

**Paper Link:**  
https://doi.org/10.1145/3411764.3445602  
https://nick-taylor.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/key_chi21_proceedwithcare.pdf

**Paper Summary:**  
This CHI 2021 paper rethinks home IoT through the lens of care. Instead of focusing only on how smart homes can become more efficient or convenient, the authors examine how domestic technologies participate in care, maintenance, responsibility, power, and relationships. The paper argues that care is not a one-way act of "devices caring for users." Rather, it is embedded in practices involving households, objects, environments, and social relations. Smart home design must consider who is cared for, who bears the cost of care, what is considered worthy of care, and how technology reshapes these relationships.

**What We Can Learn:**  
This paper supports our goal of balancing functionality and care. Device AI Identity should not simply be cute, gentle, or emotional; it should express concrete, responsible, and contextually appropriate care. For example, devices may remind users about energy consumption, air quality, or family members' states without disturbing them. At the same time, they must avoid turning care into control, discipline, or burden. The paper also reminds us that system evaluation should not only measure efficiency, but also whether users feel respected, understood, and supported.

## 7. The Technology Is Enemy for Me at the Moment: How Smart Home Technologies Assert Control Beyond Intent

**Paper Link:**  
https://doi.org/10.1145/3411764.3445058  
https://research.aalto.fi/en/publications/the-technology-is-enemy-for-me-at-the-moment-how-smart-home-techn/

**Paper Summary:**  
This CHI 2021 paper examines how smart home technologies can assert control beyond users' intentions. The authors point out that smart homes are not neutral tools that simply execute user commands. Through default settings, automation logic, access permissions, device interconnections, and remote control, they can reshape power relations within the household. In some cases, technology forces household members into new roles, such as who can control devices, who is monitored, and who must adapt to system rules.

**What We Can Learn:**  
This paper provides an important risk perspective for our AI Identity design. The more identity, initiative, and collaborative capability a device has, the more clearly we need to define controllability, transparency, and boundaries. We should not simply pursue devices that are more proactive, more personalized, or more "intelligent." We must also avoid making users feel managed, judged, or deprived of control by the system. For a multi-agent smart home, it is especially important to design mechanisms for conflict resolution, user authorization, action explanation, and override, ensuring that creativity and care do not become excessive intervention.
